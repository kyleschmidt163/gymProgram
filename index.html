Instructions on how to use the calculator ▼

What is RPE?

RPE stands for Rate of Perceived Exertion — a scale from 1 to 10 that measures how hard a set was. RPE 10 means absolute max effort (couldn't do another rep). RPE 7 means you had about 3 reps left in the tank. This calculator uses RPE to estimate your 1 rep max (e1RM) and then calculate working weights for each week.

Entering your lifts

  1. Select your program from the dropdown
  2. Enter the weight, reps and RPE from a recent working set for each lift — it doesn't have to be a max, just an honest effort
  3. The calculator will estimate your 1RM and display it under each lift
  4. Fill in any optional lifts if you have numbers — these unlock precise accessory weights. If left blank, the calculator falls back to a percentage of your main lift e1RM:
    • RDL → Deadlift × 80%
    • Larsen Press → Bench × 90%
    • Front Squat / High Bar → Squat × 85%
    • Pause Deadlift → Deadlift × 80%
    • Pause Squat → Squat × 80%
    • Bent Over Row → no fallback, weight omitted if blank
  5. For the Advanced RPE program, you can optionally enter your Pause Deadlift and Pause Squat numbers — these unlock precise weights for those movements. Without them, the calculator uses −20% of your main lift e1RM as a fallback

Program options

Training Days — 4-day splits include a variation/leg day. 3-day combines or drops that day.

Target Rep Range — sets the rep target for your main compound top sets across the program. The Advanced RPE program uses this to drive all variation and accessory rep counts automatically.

Progression Goal — Week 4 either goes AMRAP (as many reps as possible at the calculated weight) or adds +2.5kg to the top set.

Lift substitutions

The Lift Substitutions panel sits below the main lifts section and shows only exercises relevant to your selected program and day count. Pick an alternative from the dropdown before generating — the output (and the secondary lift input labels) will update to match. Some replacements like Hamstring Curls and Single Leg RDL will omit calculated weight from the output since they don't track the same way. The full list of alternatives is in the FAQ.

Reading the output

Each week is collapsible — tap the week header to expand it. Exercises are colour coded:

  1. Blue — main compound top sets
  2. Faded blue — back-off sets (same lift, lower weight)
  3. Green / Accent — secondary compounds (calculated)
  4. Muted — accessories and supersets (fixed volume, adjust weight to feel)

Week structure

Most programs run 4 working weeks with RPE climbing 7 → 8 → 8.5 → 9, followed by a deload week at RPE 6. The deload is not optional — it's where adaptation happens.

Printing / saving

Once a program is generated, use the Print / Save as PDF button to get a clean printable version. On mobile you can save this as a PDF to your files.

What to run next after Week 5 ▼

You've finished the 5-week block — what now?

Completing Week 5 (the deload) means you've run the full training cycle at your chosen rep range. At this point you have two paths forward, and the right choice depends on how Week 4 felt and what your goals are.

Option 1 — Drop the rep range and go heavier (Strength Progression)

If Week 4 at your current rep range felt strong and manageable, it's time to progress. Select a new rep range that is 2 reps lower than your previous one (e.g. move from 8 reps → 6 reps, or 6 reps → 4 reps), recalculate your program with your current numbers, and run the full 5-week block again. This is how you cycle from a hypertrophy-focused block toward peak strength — each rep range down builds more intensity and specificity for competition or maximal lifts.

  1. Log your best recent set (ideally from Week 3 or 4)
  2. Open the calculator, select the same program, reduce the rep range by 2
  3. Enter your updated numbers and regenerate

Option 2 — Return to 8 reps and run a new hypertrophy block

If you've already stepped down through the rep ranges (or if you hit a plateau), reset back to 8 reps and re-run the hypertrophy block. By the time you return to 8 reps, your 1RM will have increased from the previous cycles, so the weights will be heavier than your last hypertrophy block — this is progressive overload working as intended. Use your most recent working set to recalculate.

General rule

The progression ladder looks like this: 8 reps → 6 reps → 4 reps → (optional) 2–3 reps → back to 8 reps. Each time you complete the ladder and return to 8, your baseline strength should be meaningfully higher. Don't rush to peel the rep range — it's more important that each week of each block feels like genuine work at the prescribed RPE than it is to hurry into the next phase.

Frequently Asked Questions ▼

Q: What do I do if I don't want to perform a certain exercise (e.g. Front Squats)?

Use the Lift Substitutions panel below the main lifts section — it shows dropdowns for each substitutable exercise in your selected program. Pick your alternative before generating and it'll be swapped in automatically. The full list of alternatives (best → worst case):

  1. Front Squat → Safety Bar Squat → High Bar Squat (worst case)
  2. RDL → Stiff Legged Deadlift → Pause Deadlifts → Hamstring Curls → Single Leg RDL (worst case — no calculated weight)
  3. Larsen Press → Pause Bench Press → Close Grip Bench Press
  4. Pendlay Row → Bent Over Row → DB Row → Machine Row (worst case)
  5. Pull Ups → Lat Pulldowns → Chin Ups → Assisted Pull Ups (worst case)
  6. Barbell Curl → Incline DB Curl → Standing Alternating DB Curl → Concentration Curl (worst case)
  7. Skullcrushers (BB) → Skullcrushers (DB) → Rope Pressdown → Single Arm Rope Pressdown → Dips (worst case — too labour intensive)
  8. Shoulder Shrugs (BB) → Shoulder Shrugs (DB) → Upright Row (worst case)
  9. Cable Fly → DB Fly → Pec Deck → Push Ups (worst case)

Q: I am sick, what should I do?

If it's the flu, stay home. If it's a cold, it's up to you if you want to be an asshole and spread it but you're fine to train.

Q: I am injured — it's not major but I need to skip some exercises?

So do it. Progress where you can and save yourself trying to catch up everything later. Work around injuries but don't stop entirely. If your shoulder is busted, think of ways to keep yourself moving without pain. As long as you can train other areas with minor pain you can push through. But see a physio obviously if it's lingering longer than two weeks.

If you're inflamed, try green lipid mussel extract. I use meloxicam — a prescription anti-inflammatory drug — sparingly when given by a doctor every few months. Joint pain will happen but is minimised with RPE adjustments.

Q: I can't make the session — I'm too busy, blah blah

So make it up the next day. If it's too delayed, make it up the next week by repeating the session you would have done last week. You can still hit an AMRAP in Week 4 and may surprise yourself by a new rep range in that calculation.

Q: I am wrecked this week, how can I still progress?

Drop the assistance work and focus on hitting your top compound lifts for the week. If you drive them forward everything else will fall into place.

Q: I am a total noob — I don't know what weights to use or where to start?

Start with whatever weight you can comfortably perform for 12 reps. Everything else will fall into place — move up once you are consistently hitting that weight and can progress.

This also applies for major compounds. If you want to really ensure you're okay though, I suggest hitting up a friend that lifts to take you through major compound lifts like deadlifts. If you actually know me IRL I will help you of course — just send me a message on Instagram saying you wanna hit the gym, can you shit on my form. Ez.

Q: Should I film my sets?

If you can, you absolutely should.

Q: What supplements should I use?

Creatine. Everything else is contextual to your diet and micro-nutrient gaps. Everything else is a waste of time.
